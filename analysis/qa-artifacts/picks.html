Top 5 Large-Cap Picks

The tape just told you something important: Micron ripped and chips staged what Cramer called a "revenge trade" for last week's misguided selling, while the Dow closed at a record on revived AI optimism. But Morgan Stanley is flagging a rotation away from chipmakers into hyperscalers as the AI trade matures — that's the tension I'm underwriting across this list.

1. AVGO — Broadcom Inc. The consensus view is Broadcom is a chip stock; the mispricing is that it's really a hyperscaler-capex derivative with better margins than any hyperscaler itself. Operating margin of 40.8% and EV/EBITDA of 49.4x look rich against a 27.0x forward P/E equivalent, but that's the price of 36.2% net margins and a business increasingly levered to custom AI silicon (XPUs) rather than merchant GPU cyclicality. Shares are up 3.7% on the day as the chip complex snaps back. The variant view: as Morgan Stanley flags a pivot to hyperscalers, Broadcom sits at the intersection — it monopolizes the custom-silicon supply chain those same hyperscalers depend on. Risk: EV-to-sales of 27x leaves zero room for a slowdown in custom ASIC orders from the two or three customers that dominate that book.

2. META — Meta Platforms, Inc. Meta trades at 28.1x earnings with a 30.2% net margin and 30.2% ROE — cheaper on a P/E basis than Broadcom or Tesla despite comparable AI-capex torque. EV/EBITDA of 17.1x is the cheapest multiple of any mega-cap AI compounder in this list. The stock is up 3.0% today riding the same AI-optimism wave. The overlooked angle: Meta is a net beneficiary, not a victim, of the "Big Tech pocketing the AI equity" debate raised in this MarketWatch piece — regulatory noise around data ownership is a tail risk, not a base case. Risk: FCF-to-OCF of just 0.40 shows capex intensity (reality-lab and AI infra spend) is eating a huge chunk of operating cash flow; any capex guide-up compresses the free-cash-flow story further.

3. TSLA — Tesla, Inc. This is the highest-risk, highest-torque name on the list and I'm ranking it here purely on momentum plus optionality, not on fundamentals — at 418x trailing earnings and 254.9x free cash flow, valuation offers no margin of safety. But the stock is up 6.7% today, the single largest mover among mega-caps, and MarketWatch flags Tesla/SpaceX-related volatility now exceeding the Nasdaq-100's own historical vol — that's a signal this name is trading on catalyst-driven repricing, not fundamentals. Operating margin is a thin 5.1%, net margin 4.0% — the market is paying for optionality on robotaxi/AI, not the auto business. Risk: any miss on autonomy timelines or margin compression in the core EV business (already thin at 18.0% gross margin) triggers a violent multiple reset.

4. ORCL — Oracle Corporation Oracle's book is a house-of-cards on the surface — ROE of 53.4% only because the equity base is thin, and free cash flow is negative (FCF-to-OCF ratio of -0.74) due to heavyweight AI-data-center capex of $19.10/share. Stock is up 2.5% today. The variant call: Oracle's EV/EBITDA of 21.9x is a full turn cheaper than Broadcom's and in line with legacy-software peers, despite Oracle now running one of the largest AI-cloud infrastructure backlogs in the industry (OCI). The market is still pricing this as a database company, not a hyperscaler challenger. Risk: negative free cash flow means the AI infrastructure bet has to pay off on schedule — if cloud bookings decelerate, the balance sheet (interest debt per share of $85.8M scaled) gets uncomfortable fast.

5. JPM — JP Morgan Chase & Co. The only non-tech name here, included because it's the cheapest quality compounder on the list: 16.2x earnings, 2.6x price-to-book, and a 9.0% dividend yield-adjusted return profile most AI names can't touch. Up 1.4% today in a rally where stocks are also getting a seasonal tailwind as Congress recesses. With bitcoin rebounding on Trump's crypto comments and Klarna pushing into a U.S. bank charter, the financial-services landscape is shifting — JPM is the incumbent best positioned to absorb fintech disruption rather than be disrupted by it, given its scale in payments and capital markets. Risk: ROA of just 1.3% reflects a heavily levered balance sheet; any credit-cycle turn hits net income disproportionately.

Top 5 Small-Cap & Growth Picks

1. LMND — Lemonade, Inc. The standout mover of the day, up 10.0% to $78.70 on a $6.05B market cap. Lemonade is an AI-underwriting insurtech finally scaling — this is the name Morgan Stanley's "hyperscaler pivot" thesis quietly supports, since Lemonade's moat is proprietary claims-AI, not GPU access. The risk/reward here is a momentum breakout on top of a business inflecting toward profitability; the catalyst is the stock's outsized reaction versus a broad tape that was only up 0.7-1.1% on the major indices. Risk: still a story stock — no profitability metrics in this list, so any deceleration in premium growth or loss-ratio miss reverses the move fast.

2. ACHR — Archer Aviation Inc. Up 7.8% to $5.37 on a $4.1B market cap, riding the same eVTOL enthusiasm lifting Joby. This sits adjacent to the SpaceX/aerospace thematic already getting attention — Yahoo Finance flagged SpaceX's next catalyst driving renewed interest in the whole next-gen aerospace complex today. Archer is a pure-play regulatory-catalyst name: certification progress moves this stock double digits in a session. Risk: pre-revenue at scale, cash-burn dependent, and any FAA timeline slippage is a violent de-rating catalyst in the other direction.

3. INDI — indie Semiconductor, Inc. Up 6.9% to $4.63, a sub-$1B microcap semiconductor name riding the same chip-sector "return to optimism" that lifted Micron today, per MarketWatch. Indie is a pure-play auto-semiconductor name (ADAS, sensor fusion) — smallest-cap, highest-beta way to play the same chip re-rating driving the mega-cap semi names in the portfolio higher, without paying Nvidia/Broadcom multiples. Risk: extremely thin float and market cap means any broad tech de-risking hits this name disproportionately hard; auto-semi demand is also cyclical and exposed to EV production cuts.

4. SHLS — Shoals Technologies Group, Inc. Up 6.3% to $10.58, a $1.8B solar-electrical-BOS name. This is a torque play on the renewables case getting strengthened by the Iran war — geopolitical risk premium on oil is pushing capital back toward solar/storage infrastructure names that got left for dead in the recent rate-driven selloff. Shoals makes the electrical balance-of-system components for utility-scale solar — high operating leverage to any reacceleration in utility-scale solar bookings. Risk: rate-sensitive capex cycle; if long rates back up again, project financing for utility solar slows and this reverses.

5. FLNC — Fluence Energy, Inc. Up 4.8% to $17.82, a $3.3B energy-storage integrator — the direct read-through from the same Iran war / renewables-and-storage thesis benefiting Shoals, but Fluence sits closer to the grid-storage side of the AI-power-demand story — data centers are driving grid-storage buildout as much as solar is. This is a smaller, higher-beta way to play the same electrical-infrastructure theme as Vertiv without paying Vertiv's 29.4x EV/EBITDA. Risk: Fluence has a history of margin volatility on fixed-price contracts; any commodity input cost spike (battery cells, steel) compresses margins quickly.
